---
title: "Symplectic Euler and why the ideal-gas sim runs velocity Verlet"
date: 2026-04-18

---

A short note, prompted by switching the ideal-gas simulation on the
Things page from symplectic Euler to velocity Verlet.

For a separable Hamiltonian $H(q, p) = T(p) + V(q)$, symplectic Euler
is the update

$$
p_{n+1} = p_n - \Delta t\, \nabla V(q_n), \qquad
q_{n+1} = q_n + \Delta t\, \nabla T(p_{n+1}).
$$

It is the composition of two exact flows — the drift generated by $T$
followed by the kick from $V$ — so it is a symplectic map, and by
backward-error analysis it exactly conserves a *modified* Hamiltonian
$\tilde{H} = H + \Delta t\, H_1 + \Delta t^2\, H_2 + \cdots$. The true
energy $H$ therefore oscillates around its initial value rather than
drifting, which is the property that matters for long runs. Contrast
explicit Euler, which is not symplectic and leaks energy monotonically
in either direction depending on the sign of the local curvature.

The cost of symplectic Euler is that it is only first-order accurate:
the $H_1$ term in $\tilde{H}$ is $\mathcal{O}(\Delta t)$ and the error
in observables scales the same way. Velocity Verlet,

$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{n+1/2} &= p_n + \tfrac{\Delta t}{2}\, F(q_n), \\
q_{n+1}   &= q_n + \Delta t\, p_{n+1/2}/m, \\
p_{n+1}   &= p_{n+1/2} + \tfrac{\Delta t}{2}\, F(q_{n+1}),
\end{aligned}
$$

is a Strang splitting of the same two flows and is second-order
accurate while still symplectic and time-reversible. Its shadow
Hamiltonian starts at $H_2 = \mathcal{O}(\Delta t^2)$, so energy
oscillations are proportionally smaller for the same $\Delta t$.

For the ideal-gas simulation this matters because the soft-sphere
contacts are stiff: the pair stiffness $k$ fixes a collision resolution
time $\tau_c = \pi\sqrt{m/2k}$, and the ratio $\tau_c / \Delta t$ is the
number of integrator steps resolving each collision. Symplectic Euler
at the largest $\Delta t$ that keeps collisions resolved still produced
a visible secular drift in the running $k_B T/m$ diagnostic, because
the $\mathcal{O}(\Delta t)$ modified-Hamiltonian term is comparable to
the collision impulse. Velocity Verlet at the same $\Delta t$ moves that
error to $\mathcal{O}(\Delta t^2)$, bringing the drift down to the
$\sim 10^{-2}$ level visible in the $k_B T/m$ readout — a residual that
comes mostly from finite $\tau_c / \Delta t$ rather than from the
integrator. The cost is one extra force-free half-kick per step and
one extra storage buffer for $\mathbf{a}_n$; effectively free on the
GPU.
